The thesis

Price is a shadow, not a series

Most quantitative models study the shadow — the price series itself — and inherit all the noise and opacity that comes with it. Kestrel studies the thing casting the shadow: the crowd of participants whose decisions become the price. Understand the crowd, and the chart stops being a mystery and starts being an output.

01 · TWO EYES

One engine, looking both ways

A kestrel hunts on sight — hovering dead-still above a field, seeing what nothing else can, then striking. Our engine looks two ways at once. Agora looks inward, at a living market in miniature it grows from first principles and tunes until it behaves like the real thing. Pythia looks outward, reading real markets and answering with fair value and honest confidence. Each line of sight sharpens the other.

02 · READABLE

Answers you can argue with

Kestrel doesn't hand you a number and demand faith. Its conclusions come out as rules in plain language — the kind a portfolio manager can read, question, and veto. Classic risk discipline shows up in those rules on its own, without anyone writing it in. And when we distill a simulated trader whose logic we planted ourselves, the engine recovers it at 0.97 fidelity — against 0.51 for a method blind to the market's structure. Readability you can measure.

03 · DISCIPLINED

The bar is set before the test

Every claim Kestrel makes has to survive machinery built to kill it: live scoring against real outcomes, full trading costs, strict past-versus-future separation, and comparison against simple baselines. Ideas that don't clear the bar are retired in the open — which is exactly why the ones that survive mean something.

The engine

Agora and Pythia — how Kestrel sees both ways

A market is a conversation between thousands of participants with different goals, horizons, and appetites. Kestrel models the conversation, not just its transcript.

Agora — the inward eye

A synthetic market, populated by thousands of distinct participants — patient institutions, restless speculators, liquidity providers, opportunists — trading against each other in a world Kestrel controls completely. The hallmarks of real markets emerge on their own: rare violent moves, calm that turns to storm, crowding and retreat. Nobody scripts them. They emerge — which is how we know the machinery underneath is honest.

Pythia — the outward eye

Pythia reads the real tape and answers the only question that matters: what is this actually worth, and how sure are we? Her answer is never a single number — it's a fair-value estimate wrapped in a confidence range that has been checked against reality. When Pythia says 90%, we've verified it means about 90%.

The dialogue between them

Agora is tuned until its synthetic markets are statistically indistinguishable from real ones — a rehearsal space where Kestrel can run ten thousand market histories instead of the single one reality offers. What's learned in rehearsal gives Pythia her head start on the real thing; what Pythia sees in the wild flows back to make the rehearsals sharper.

Discipline

What our green lights actually mean

Anyone can show you a backtest. We built the lie-detector before we built the product — so that when something on this page is marked proven, that word is carrying real weight.

Proven — survived pre-declared tests Working — built and running Early — promising, still being validated Planned — designed, ahead of us

The test comes before the claim

Every hypothesis gets its pass/fail bar written down before the experiment runs — so a result can't be reinterpreted into a success after the fact. Earnings events, for example, earned their place in the engine by clearing a pre-declared statistical bar on real data; ideas that sound just as plausible but didn't clear it were left out. The engine contains only what survived.

A live scoreboard, not a backtest

We built machinery that logs predictions on a regulated exchange and scores them when markets settle — a grader that cannot be argued with. In a thirty-hour live run, 545 ultra-short-horizon calls landed at 49.9%: a coin flip. We retired that idea in writing and re-aimed the same machinery at longer horizons. The discipline that kills a weak signal in thirty hours is the same discipline that makes a surviving signal worth backing.

Leakage is impossible by construction

The most common way quantitative research lies is subtle: information from the future seeps into the past, and a strategy looks brilliant right up until it meets reality. Kestrel's data foundation is built so that this physically cannot happen — every model, at every moment, sees only what was knowable at that moment. It isn't a policy we follow; it's a property of the architecture.

The flagship cleared the bar

Kestrel's core strategy ranks a whole basket by how far each name sits from fair value and leans toward the bargains. In a pre-registered walk-forward on a decade of S&P 100 history — full costs on every trade, past and future kept strictly apart, measured against a benchmark harder to beat than most admit — it delivered a mean out-of-sample Sharpe near three, beat that benchmark in every fold, and reproduced when re-fit from scratch. The evidence

The flagship cleared its bar

For a long time this section would have been a promise. It isn't anymore. Kestrel's fair-value ranking strategy was put through a pre-registered walk-forward on a decade of real S&P 100 history — full trading costs charged on every trade, past and future kept strictly apart — and it held.

What made the difference

Two things the engine already knew how to do. A second, independently-trained read of each asset lifted the signal measurably — nearly for free, because Kestrel was already computing it. And the way the strategy turns its rankings into an actual book — trading a name only when it moves enough to be worth the cost — is what let a real edge survive the toll that quietly kills most of them. That toll, charged honestly, is the difference between a backtest and the truth.

What we're not claiming

This is walk-forward research, not a live track record. Stress-tested into the 2022 bear market the edge weakens — one window's signal even inverts — so it is regime-sensitive, not a law of nature. And every failed cousin is on the record beside it: daily long/short books over relationship graphs that couldn't clear costs, retired in writing. The number above is the configuration that did.

What's proven is rigorous and explainable. Most of the upside is still unpriced — and we'd rather show you exactly where the frontier is than pretend we're past it.
The flywheel

The product and the R&D engine are the same thing

Kestrel lives inside a mobile app built to feel like a game — frictionless enough that someone with no interest in markets plays it for its own sake. That isn't a growth gimmick. Every hour of play does real work: it generates validated, explainable strategy artifacts — the same R&D that sharpens the company's own edge. The person enjoying the game and the engine getting smarter are the same loop, and nothing is extracted from the user to close it.

→ ONE PATH

The fun path is the valuable path

The incentive design has a spine: the best move for even a pure-dopamine grinder is to produce genuinely good intelligence. No "just gamble" shortcut, no "trade more" nudge — because we refuse to build them. No loss-aversion, no manufactured urgency, no loot boxes. The addictive path and the valuable path are deliberately the same path.

→ THREE CURRENCIES

Intelligence is the currency that matters

Soft usage credits and hard dollars, with a deliberate gap between them: credits never convert to cash directly. The only bridge is spending them to produce intelligence — a validated, distilled, executable strategy — and that is what carries real value. The third, implicit currency closes the loop honestly.

→ IT COMPOUNDS

Play becomes a moat

Every distillation that clears the honest gate is a permanent asset — and it makes the next one easier to find. The same activity that delights the user compounds the engine's edge and, along the way, surfaces the people who are unusually good at it. Product, R&D, and recruiting, out of a single loop.

The horizon

Markets are the first surface, not the last

What Kestrel actually learns is more general than any one market: how a population of decision-makers, each acting on partial information, drives a system toward an outcome. Exchange order books are simply the cleanest place to begin. Feed the same engine richer inputs and the world it can model — and price — widens dramatically. Every new kind of data isn't a new product to build from scratch; it's a new sense for an engine that already knows what to do with it.

→ THE HUMAN GRAPH

Who moves whom

Behind every price is a web of people — the relationships, allegiances, and overlapping histories among the decision-makers who steer companies and capital. Mapped as structure the engine can read, that web becomes a modelable force, not background noise.

→ LANGUAGE AT SCALE

Information, the moment it's information

The world announces itself in words first — filings, headlines, narrative, sentiment. An engine that ingests that flow as it breaks reacts to what's changed the instant it changes, rather than waiting for it to show up in the tape.

→ FRONTIER REASONING

The best models, in the loop

The most capable reasoning systems available, integrated as first-class participants — reading, connecting, and forming hypotheses alongside the simulation. Kestrel is built to put their judgement to work inside the engine, not merely beside it.

Each of these is another current feeding the same river: one cohesive engine that turns more of the world into something it can simulate, anticipate, and act on. Markets were the proving ground. The ambition is a general capital engine — and its foundation is deliberately built to grow into exactly that.

Where it's going

One engine, several products

Kestrel wasn't built for a single trade. The same fitted engine prices event contracts, ranks assets by fair value, and powers tools people can put their hands on.

Forge · on device

A strategy you can read, in your hand

A trading rule becomes a graph: features and constants flow into comparisons, comparisons into AND/OR logic, and everything into a single TRADE SIGNAL. Drag to build it by hand, or ask the on-device model to improve it and accept the change as a before/after diff. Every edit is re-scored on held-out data with honest costs included — the same engine as the desktop stack, running on the phone.

Working · on device

Forge — the strategy workbench

A visual editor where a trading rule is a graph you build and understand — drag nodes by hand, or ask the on-device model to improve a rule and watch the change arrive as a before/after diff you accept or reject. Every edit is scored on held-out data with honest costs included; custom indicators are first-class and reusable. Readable by design, all the way down.

Planned

Working · prototype

Planned

Named for the watchman with a hundred eyes: a daily cockpit showing picks, proposed trades, and model health, with a human approving every action before it executes. A static console preview and session-playback harness exist today; the full supervised deployment loop is designed but not shipped.

Its first real piece is already live: Forge, the on-device strategy workbench. A run-playback viewer (umarketsim-viz) and a strategy marketplace round out the picture.
